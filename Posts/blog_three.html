We Need a Bridge

Why a bridge? It's to help people cross the digital divide, which is far harder to cross than any canyon or gorge.

The discussion on why inclusivity is important in online spaces should always be preceded with the true question and topic this addresses: Why is inclusivity important? This seems like a simple question with a simple answer, but the truth is nothing is simple in the digital space. On a biological level, the sense of ‘belonging’ is important to prevent loneliness, depression, and other negative mental health effects. (Robinson S, 2019) In a more academic sense, inclusivity is important because everyone deserves to contribute to discussions that impact their lives, and to engage with others in a meaningful way in online spaces.

The question of inclusivity in the digital age could be better worded as “Can a person with no prior knowledge of websites use this website effectively?”

When we become accustomed to something, we tend to forget that not everyone has been exposed to it their entire lives. The perfect example for this is, well…the internet! For young adults, we cannot imagine what life was like before the internet existed. We see three dots and immediately know “Hey! The meatballs menu symbol means more options will appear if you click on it :)” but how would someone without this knowledge figure this out?

{Thankfully, we can look at examples in real life: the meatball menu looks just like a set of ellipses, which is typically used to mean “hey this sentence is continuing” so people might have that association with it. The discussion of how things in the digital space relate to things in the real world should be explored to promote inclusion.}

Inclusivity doesn’t just refer to age; it also refers to race, gender, religion, and other things that people perceive as ‘dividing’ us. Poverty and race often plays a role in access to broadband, or comfort levels when using technology. (Hassan, n.d.) Gender plays a role in which students can access technology. (Cooper, J., & Weaver, K.D. (2003) In conclusion, this is an ongoing issue that will only morph with time, and we need to work to make sites more comprehensible to those not tech-savvy.
