Digital Justice

The internet in no longer new. It is no longer surprising to hear that someone owns a computer that can access the internet. We live in a digital age, which means that existing societal structures are being revamped constantly to fit into the new, internet-based, technology-filled space that we operate in. This digital migration is truly fascinating as the transparency of the internet allows us to find gaps in access very easily. There are of course pros and cons to the internet as a whole, just as there are with most things in life. There is a strange grey area in the middle that often gets ignored as well. In this critical writing, I will attempt to discuss these in depth to the best of my ability. The structure of this writing will be far stranger, looser, and terribly structured than the previous writings, as this reflects the chaotic nature of the internet, and the disorganised train of thought that needs to be followed when discussing these issues.

I think the main way that the positive and negative aspects of the internet overlap is through the broadcasting of negative aspects. What exactly does that mean? Well, years ago when hate-crimes occured (they still do, of course) it was often a he said, she said situation. There was no tangible proof. Not only that, but victimes would often feel alone. The internet not only amplifies the voices of the victims and allows them to share their atories in spaces where they feel safe, but it also often punishes those responsible. The existence of online papers such as this one discussing accountability online for violence against women, or this project based around exposing and preventing hate speech online prove that victimes no longer have to feel entirely alone.

Access to the internet is a problem explored in depth (or as much depth as I am capable of) in my previous article, but it is quite an important topic and falls into this section quite well. The coronavirus-19 pandemic truly shed light on the volume of people that have no access to the internet. The UN (United Nations) declared access to the internet to be a human right in July of 2016 - which was almost exactly 6 years ago - and yet it took a pandemic that started 3 years later to prove tat this human right was not enforced. When the pandemic hit, we knew everything was going to change. This was entirely true, as three years later, the world before us has not returned to the way it once was. Many things that used to be done in person had to be adapted and placed online instead...which made the digital divide even larger. People who had no access to the internet but who used to have access to education suddenly were unable to access their classes. Students who had parents that earned a higher income appear to have been able to have handled the transition better. Government spending on computers and internet access increased significantly in an attempt to help lessen the impact of the online-learning on the lower income students, but socioeconomic pressures meant this was not enough.

For every dark cloud, there is a stream of sunlight. Though moving to online working and learning was difficult for many, this was a benefit to some. Those with disabilities who were often told that they could not work from home, were suddenly able to work at home. People with disabilities expressed online (often on Twitter) how the privleges they asked for, fought for, and were denied were suddenly entirely possible when able-bodied people needed to work from home. Though this is a dark look at how society and wthe working world disregards reasonable requests from those with disabilities and yet accepts it as reality quickly when those without disabilities require the changes, it is also a good thing that these changes could be made.

On the other hand, these same groups are now expressing fear at the idea of losing these accomodations as the world attempts to continue as if the pandemic has ended. If the world did not burn when most of us worked from home, surely this means that we can continue this way? Businesses are doubtful.
